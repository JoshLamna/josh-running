What the data says. This is a ranked list of your biggest performance limiters based on your 3 years of Garmin data, your medical situation, and where the evidence shows the most available improvement. These are in priority order — fix #1 before chasing #5.
1
Aerobic base — the engine isn't back yet
Your pace at a given HR is still 15–20 sec/km off your pre-AFib baseline for longer efforts. The ablation, reduced training, and weight gain have all shrunk your aerobic engine. This is the root cause of everything else right now. Every other metric improves when this improves.
Impact: Very High  ·  Fix: Consistent 40–50km weeks at easy–moderate effort for 12+ weeks. Add cycling as aerobic top-up.
2
No lactate threshold work in your history
Your HR zone data shows 67–79% of runs in Z3 (tempo), 8–14% in Z4, and virtually zero in Z5. You're doing a lot of moderate "grey zone" running and very little true threshold or VO2max work. This is the most impactful type of training for half marathon performance and it's been missing. Your HM PB was likely run on volume alone, not sharpness.
Impact: Very High  ·  Fix: Weekly interval session from July onwards. 6×1km at 4:30–4:45/km. Also one sweetspot ride on the bike builds VO2max without run impact.
3
Body weight — 4kg above racing weight
At 88kg vs your likely racing weight of 83–84kg, you're carrying an estimated 8–12 sec/km penalty at race pace. This is one of the most underappreciated performance factors. The good news: this returns naturally as training volume normalises. No aggressive dieting needed — it would compromise recovery.
Impact: High  ·  Fix: Let training do the work over 3–4 months. Prioritise protein, avoid processed snacks. Don't diet while rebuilding base.
4
Running economy — vertical oscillation creeping up
Your vertical oscillation rose from 8.4cm in 2023 to 8.9cm by mid-2025. Ideal is below 8.0cm. Every centimetre of unnecessary bounce is wasted energy — you're going up instead of forward. Your GCT is also elevated post-ablation (265ms vs 250ms at peak). These are linked: lower bounce usually means shorter ground contact.
Impact: Medium  ·  Fix: Add 6×20 sec strides once a week — fast, light, cadence-focused. Drills: high knees, A-skips. Think "quiet feet" on easy runs. Cadence target: 174–178 spm.
5
Cycling base — very early stage
Your indoor cycling NP of 80–98W is a beginner-level output. As your primary aerobic supplement, it needs to develop into a genuine training tool, not just cross-training. Currently it's not adding much aerobic stimulus. Within 3–4 months of consistent Zone 2 work, your NP should reach 130–150W at the same HR — and that's where it starts meaningfully contributing to your running engine.
Impact: Medium (rising)  ·  Fix: Two rides/week minimum. One pure Zone 2 (45–60 min, conversational HR). One sweetspot or short interval ride. Track NP monthly — it should rise 5–10W per month early on.
6
Strength and injury resilience — no data, likely a gap
Your Garmin logs show no strength or gym sessions. At 629 runs and 6,844km with no recorded strength work, you've built impressive volume but likely have muscular imbalances that will become limiters as you push harder. Glutes, hip flexors, and single-leg stability are the usual weak points for high-volume runners. This becomes especially relevant when you add speedwork.
Impact: Medium  ·  Fix: 2×20 min per week. Single-leg squats, hip thrusts, calf raises, Copenhagen planks. Add before or after easy runs — no gym needed.
7
Race experience at shorter distances — unexplored territory
You have no standalone 5km or 10km race data. These distances develop speed-endurance and neuromuscular sharpness that transfers directly to half marathon performance. You've been a high-volume marathoner without the race sharpness that shorter distances provide. Your 7.66km at 3:55/km suggests real speed — it just hasn't been channelled into race-specific efforts.
Impact: Medium-Low  ·  Fix: Race a 10km in September 2026. It's a fitness test, a PB by definition, and will reveal your current true aerobic ceiling better than any training run.

What the HR zones reveal — too much grey zone running

Z1 Recovery Z2 Aerobic Z3 Tempo/Grey Z4 Threshold

The optimal distribution for HM training is roughly: 70–80% easy (Z1–Z2), 10–15% threshold (Z4), 5–10% VO2max (Z5). You're currently heavy in Z3 — the "junk miles" zone that's too hard to be recovery and too easy to build race sharpness. Polarising your training (easier easy days, harder hard days) is the single biggest structural change you can make.

The goal: Half marathon PB attempt by spring 2027. Sub-1:29 is achievable. The 21km long run cap does not constrain HM prep. At 31, your best running is ahead of you.
